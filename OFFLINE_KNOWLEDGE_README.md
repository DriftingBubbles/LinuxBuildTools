# Offline Knowledge Continuity Guide

When internet access is disrupted for any reason (natural disaster, infrastructure outage, conflict, or policy restrictions), communities need trusted offline knowledge to stay safe and coordinated.

Maintaining and sharing practical information in advance helps protect health, reduce panic, and improve recovery speed.

## Why this matters

If connectivity is unavailable, people still need to:
- treat common illnesses and know when emergency care is needed,
- find safe water and sanitation methods,
- communicate with neighbors and response teams,
- preserve learning materials for children and adults,
- maintain food, shelter, and basic survival routines.

A local offline knowledge library can support households, schools, clinics, and community groups during these gaps.

## Priority knowledge areas

## 1) Medical and public health

Keep offline references for:
- basic first aid,
- infection prevention and control,
- maternal and child health basics,
- vaccine and disease guidance,
- emergency warning signs requiring higher-level care.

Public sources:
- World Health Organization (WHO): https://www.who.int/
- U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC): https://www.cdc.gov/
- Médecins Sans Frontières (MSF) medical guidelines/resources: https://medicalguidelines.msf.org/

## 2) Survival and emergency preparedness

Keep offline references for:
- emergency planning checklists,
- shelter-in-place and evacuation basics,
- food storage and safe preparation,
- disaster-specific safety procedures.

Public sources:
- Ready.gov (FEMA public preparedness guidance): https://www.ready.gov/
- International Federation of Red Cross and Red Crescent Societies (IFRC): https://www.ifrc.org/
- Sphere Handbook (humanitarian standards): https://spherestandards.org/handbook/

## 3) Water, sanitation, and hygiene (WASH)

Keep offline references for:
- safe water treatment options,
- sanitation setup and maintenance,
- hygiene practices that reduce disease spread,
- waste handling and contamination prevention.

Public sources:
- WHO WASH resources: https://www.who.int/teams/environment-climate-change-and-health/water-sanitation-and-health
- UNICEF WASH: https://www.unicef.org/wash
- CDC Water, Sanitation & Hygiene-related guidance: https://www.cdc.gov/healthywater/

## 4) Communication and coordination

Keep offline references for:
- radio communication basics,
- community message formats and contact trees,
- incident logging and handoff procedures,
- plain-language emergency communication templates.

Public sources:
- International Telecommunication Union (ITU): https://www.itu.int/
- U.S. FEMA communications resources: https://www.fema.gov/
- ARRL (amateur radio emergency resources): https://www.arrl.org/public-service

## 5) Education continuity

Keep offline references for:
- core literacy and numeracy materials,
- science and health education,
- teacher guides and self-study packs,
- multilingual and age-appropriate learning resources.

Public sources:
- UNESCO education resources: https://www.unesco.org/en/education
- UNICEF learning resources: https://www.unicef.org/education
- Wikipedia (open encyclopedia, license varies by content): https://www.wikipedia.org/
- Wikiversity (open learning resources): https://www.wikiversity.org/
- Project Gutenberg (public domain books): https://www.gutenberg.org/

## 6) Spirituality and cultural resilience

Spiritual and reflective resources can support mental resilience, ethical guidance,
and community cohesion during prolonged outages and emergencies.

Keep offline references for:
- faith and spiritual texts that are important to your community,
- inclusive reflection and mental well-being materials,
- culturally important stories, songs, and traditions,
- community-led guidance documents for respectful shared spaces.

Public sources:
- Internet Archive (texts and audio collections): https://archive.org/
- Project Gutenberg (public domain religious/classical works): https://www.gutenberg.org/
- Wikisource (historical and source texts): https://wikisource.org/

## Recommended local practice

1. Build an offline content set before emergencies.
2. Keep files in open formats (PDF, HTML, EPUB, plain text).
3. Store duplicate copies on multiple devices/media.
4. Keep a source + license record for each resource bundle.
5. Review and refresh materials on a regular schedule.
